Strategic Spectrum Refarming for 5G Deployment

A Comprehensive Technical Framework for National Digital Transformation

1. Executive Summary & Research Objectives

This research evaluates the critical transition from legacy mobile networks (2G/3G/CDMA) to the 5G New Radio (NR) ecosystem. As Sri Lanka transitions toward a digital economy, spectral efficiency is the bottleneck. The objective is to provide a technical roadmap for operators to optimize the current frequency assignments.

  • Spectrum Optimization: Repurposing the 850MHz (CDMA) and 2100MHz (3G) bands for 5G Sub-6GHz coverage and capacity.
  • Infrastructure Maturity: Transitioning from 4G-Non-Standalone (NSA) to 5G-Standalone (SA) architecture.
  • Technological Deployment: Utilizing Massive MIMO, beamforming, and small-cell densities.
  • Regulatory Alignment: Harmonizing with TRCSL NFAT and ITU Region 3 standards.

2. Spectrum Refarming: The Technical Logic

Refarming is not merely switching technology; it is a complex management of interference and user migration. By phasing out CDMA and 3G, we liberate contiguous bandwidth blocks essential for high-throughput 5G.

Spectrum refarming in the Sri Lankan context is a strategic necessity to overcome spectral scarcity and optimize the migration from legacy 2G/3G networks to the 5G New Radio (NR) ecosystem. In Sri Lanka, major operators like Dialog Axiata and SLT-Mobitel have been instrumental in this transition.

The refarming logic follows a strict "Sunset & Migrate" policy:

  • Spectral Efficiency & Technology Neutrality: Operators utilize TRCSL's technology-neutral licenses to convert bands previously allocated for 3G (like 2100MHz) into 5G TDD-NR. By reducing the carrier bandwidth of legacy 3G services, they free up contiguous blocks essential for 5G NR, which requires wider channels (up to 100MHz) to deliver multi-gigabit throughput.
  • Operator Strategy (Dialog & Mobitel): Dialog and Mobitel have initiated phased decommissioning of CDMA and 3G networks. This is done via Dynamic Spectrum Sharing (DSS), where 4G LTE and 5G NR frequencies are shared dynamically in real-time. This ensures that users on older devices are not disconnected while the network capacity is repurposed for 5G.
  • Interference Management & Guard Bands: A critical technical challenge is managing adjacent channel interference. Refarming requires precision filtering and the optimization of guard bands to prevent signal leakage between the refarmed 5G bands and existing 4G LTE deployments.
  • User Migration Roadmap: Successful refarming relies on the aggressive promotion of 4G/5G enabled VoLTE devices. By incentivizing the population to move away from legacy handsets, operators reduce the signaling overhead required to maintain 3G/CDMA networks, effectively shifting that energy consumption and bandwidth to high-efficiency 5G infrastructure.

This evolution is not just about speed; it is about maximizing the bits-per-hertz efficiency of every megahertz of spectrum assigned to the operators.

4. Economic & Industrial Impact

Figure 4: The 5G Infrastructure Sharing Model. Integrating Fiber-to-the-Antenna (FTTA) in local contexts.

5G will serve as the backbone for national economic evolution. Key verticals include:

  • Dark Manufacturing (Industry 4.0): Replacing manual assembly lines with autonomous robotic arms coordinated via 5G Ultra-Reliable Low-Latency Communication (URLLC).
  • Telemedicine: Enabling real-time diagnostic imaging and remote robotic surgery, bridging the gap between rural patients and metropolitan specialists.
  • Autonomous Transport: Vehicular-to-Everything (V2X) communication to optimize traffic flow in Colombo and major urban centers.
  • Smart Energy Grids (IoT): Massively scalable IoT connectivity to optimize national power distribution.

5. Implementation Protocols

Operators must adhere to a phased transition:

  1. Device Migration: Incentivizing customers to swap 3G devices for 4G/5G VoLTE capable terminals.
  2. Dynamic Spectrum Sharing (DSS): Temporarily allowing 4G and 5G to coexist on the same band via software management to minimize disruption during the transition.
  3. Backhaul Hardening: Converting legacy microwave links to fiber-optic backhaul to handle the 10Gbps+ throughput requirement of the 5G Core.
