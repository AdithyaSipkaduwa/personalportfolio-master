IDENTIFICATION AND INVESTIGATION OF AFLATOXIN PRESENCE AND VARIETIES IN CORN FLOUR

A portable, 2-stage semi-automatic optoelectronic system engineered for field deployment. Combining spectroscopy methodologies, precise CNC scanning, and custom STM32 firmware to execute rapid, laboratory-grade food safety diagnostics in agricultural zones.

Fungal Mycotoxins & Food Safety Rigor

Aflatoxins represent a group of highly toxic, carcinogenic secondary metabolites synthesized predominantly by Aspergillus flavus molds. These fungal pathogens typically compromise agricultural yields under volatile thermal and high-humidity storage states. Reflecting their severe health implications, the World Health Organization (WHO) mandates a rigid safety ceiling of 20 ppb (parts per billion) for consumer food stocks.

Demographic Vulnerability & Diagnostics

Maize and derived flour derivatives constitute a substantial portion of staple diets globally, serving as primary ingredients in nutritional formulations engineered for sensitive demographics, notably infants, toddlers, and expectant mothers. Addressing this critical screening bottleneck, a specialized optoelectronic verification platform was developed to execute automated variety identification and rapid toxic concentration checks.

Aflatoxin Microorganism & UV Exposure Concept

Photoluminescence & Spectroscopy Theory

At the core of this system is advanced spectroscopy methodology. When exposed to Ultraviolet (UV) light, aflatoxin molecules absorb energy, causing their electrons to elevate to a higher, unstable orbital state.

As these electrons return to their stable ground state, they release energy in the form of photons. This UV Fluorescence manifests as specific visible colors: Aflatoxin-B emits Blue light (indicating a higher risk factor), while Aflatoxin-G emits Green light.

These minuscule photon emissions are captured utilizing photo-electrostatic theory, translating light intensity into readable electronic signals via linear imaging arrays.

Stage 1: Fluorescence Identification & CNC Scanning

Sony ILX555K Linear CCD Integration

To capture the extremely faint light emitted by the aflatoxin spores, the Sony ILX555K RGB-capable CCD sensor was selected. According to the datasheet, this sensor typically operates at a 1-5MHz master clock. However, to capture "tiny light", the shutter (exposure) time had to be significantly increased.

By carefully analyzing the timing diagrams, rise times, and falling times, I successfully down-converted the two master clocks and the 4 synchronized input signals (Ø1, Ø2, RS, ROG) to 500kHz, pushing the sensor beyond its standard operational bounds for maximum light sensitivity.

STM32F411CEU6 Firmware & ADC Optimization

Generating highly stable, synchronized clock signals is a severe challenge. The STM32F411CEU6 controller was selected. Its ability to easily configure the system clock to 100MHz allowed for flawless generation of the required 500kHz signals using advanced timer features.

Overcoming Limitations: The CCD outputs 3 analog signals (RGB). Reading all three simultaneously via ADC at high speeds caused resolution drops. The firmware was customized to execute a Synchronous Color-Multiplexing Algorithm: the system reads only ONE color per sweep, completely bypassing ADC limitations and maximizing resolution.

CNC Flatbed Scanning Mechanism in Dark Chamber

Electromechanical Flatbed Scanning

Because full image processing wasn't required (only detecting Blue vs. Green intensities), a custom Mechanical CNC Scanning System was built inside a dark chamber (to block ambient light).

  • Drive System: PK268E2 Stepper Motor controlled by an A4988 motor driver.
  • Scanning Logic: Motor rotates in strict synchronization with the CCD scan process.
  • Process: The CCD moves forward scanning exclusively for Blue. It then reverses, scanning for Green.

Stage 2: Quantification via Beer-Lambert Law

Sample Preparation & Optical Measurement

To ensure accurate optical readings, raw corn samples and controlled fungal cultures underwent a rigorous laboratory preparation protocol before UV analysis.

  • Fungal Culturing: Pure strains of Aflatoxin-producing fungi (Aspergillus flavus) were carefully cultured on Potato Dextrose Agar (PDA) media at the OUSL Botany Lab. Incubation parameters were strictly controlled to establish baseline toxicity references.
  • Extraction Process: Infected corn samples were finely ground, dissolved in an Ethanol-based solvent to isolate the mycotoxins, and deeply filtered to remove solid particulates.
  • Optical Scanning: The clear, filtered extract is placed in a Quartz test tube. UV light is passed through it, and a GUVA-S12SD UV Sensor measures the transmitted light intensity.
Beer-Lambert Absorbance Model: A = ε · c · l

Quantification: Utilizing the Beer-Lambert Law, the precise absorbance (A) is calculated. By comparing this against the PDA-cultured calibration data, the exact Aflatoxin concentration in the sample is determined.

Conclusion

The primary objective of this research was to address the critical need for a portable, cost-effective method for detecting aflatoxin in Corn products. Through the integration of CCD-based fluorescence imaging and Beer–Lambert-based absorbance spectroscopy, this project successfully demonstrated the functional feasibility of a two-stage optoelectronic system. By utilizing the STM32F4 microcontroller to generate precise, synchronized signals for the Sony ILX555K CCD array, this research successfully established a functional baseline for identifying toxin presence. Currently, the system operates across a detection range of 190 ppm to 100,000 ppm. While the current prototype validates the fundamental operating principle, bridging the gap to the WHO-mandated safety threshold of 20 ppb requires a transition toward higher-resolution instrumentation. This work provides the technical blueprint for that transition, confirming that by optimizing the optical path and increasing the resolution of the data acquisition chain, the platform can evolve from an experimental sensing model into a robust, field-deployable diagnostic tool for ensuring food safety.

Future Implementations

  • Hyperspectral Analysis: • The current detection range is limited by the bit-depth of the Analog-to-Digital Converter. Transitioning to a 24-bit (or higher) ADC architecture is essential. This upgrade will significantly decrease the quantization error, allowing the system to resolve the minute changes in light intensity required to detect concentrations at the parts-per-billion (ppb) level, moving well beyond the current ppm-based detection limits. • To overcome signal attenuation at low aflatoxin concentrations, the integration of a prism and convex lens assembly is proposed. This will allow for the precise focus and diffraction of emitted fluorescence onto the CCD sensor, effectively increasing the signal-to-noise ratio and ensuring that the tiny light photons emitted by low-concentration samples are captured with higher fidelity. • By pairing the enhanced 24-bit ADC with a more advanced, high-linearity UV index sensor, the system will achieve greater accuracy in measuring absorbance. This ensures that the Beer–Lambert Law calculations are grounded in high-resolution data, minimizing experimental drift and improving the reliability of the concentration estimates. • Future implementation of narrow-band UV filters will isolate specific excitation/emission wavelengths for Aflatoxin B and G varieties. This will further reduce ambient noise, allowing the CCD to distinguish target emissions from background interference more effectively.
