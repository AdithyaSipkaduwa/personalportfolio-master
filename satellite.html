BIRDS-X Dragonfly

Deploying Sri Lanka's 3rd Nano-Satellite Ground Tracking Node

1. BIRDS-X Dragonfly Project Paradigm

The BIRDS-X Dragonfly is a 2U Nano-Satellite engineered as a joint global venture by the Kyushu Institute of Technology (Kyutech), Japan, and the Arthur C. Clarke Institute for Modern Technologies (ACCIMT), Sri Lanka. Carried to the International Space Station (ISS) aboard SpaceX mission SPX-33 on August 24, 2025, it marks a critical developmental shift in Sri Lanka's aerospace capabilities following RAAVANA-1 (2019) and KITSUNE (2022).

The core academic goals focus on evaluating cutting-edge low-cost Commercial Off-The-Shelf (COTS) components inside space environments, operating an Automatic Packet Reporting System (APRS) Digipeater for global amateur radio relays, and deploying Store-and-Forward data network routing systems.

2. Ground Station RF Array & Kinematics

Tracking Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellites requires high-precision structural tracking networks. The ground station array deployed for this operation uses a dual-band split structure split into independent VHF (Very High Frequency) and UHF (Ultra High Frequency) elements constructed from highly conductive, lightweight aerospace aluminum alloy booms.

Dual Axis Rotational Mechanics

To follow the satellite's orbital trace from horizon to horizon, the antenna array is manipulated by an autonomous dual-motor positioning architecture executing real-time target path alignments based on calculated Two-Line Element (TLE) positional data vectors:

  • Azimuth Tracking Axis: Drives horizontal rotation spanning a full range of 0° to 360°.
  • Elevation Tracking Axis: Compensates for vertical curvature variations ranging from 0° to 90° reference to local horizon points.
  • Rotational Dynamics: Controlled dynamically through variable motor angular velocities matched against orbital tracking speed vectors to prevent signal degradation or cross-polarization dropouts.

Operational Parameters from Link Budget Files

According to verified project telemetry sheets, the link budget structures for the respective bands are configured as follows:

  • Uplink Path (VHF Band): Operating at 145.825 MHz utilizing AFSK modulation with AX.25 protocol configurations at a data rate of 1200 bps. The Ground Station outputs a Transmitter Power of 50.0 W (17.0 dBW), driving an antenna system with a net Gain of 16.0 dBi to achieve a System Link Margin of 16.6 dB.
  • Downlink Path (UHF Band): Operates at 437.375 MHz using GMSK / Morse Code modulations at 4800 bps. Spacecraft transmitter operates at 0.8 W (-1.0 dBW), through a 22.0 dBi Ground Station Antenna gain, maintaining a stable Downlink Margin of 4.2 dB.

4. Hardware Calibration & Impedance Optimization

During the post-mounting integration phases, the coaxial transmission routing network—interconnected using heavy-duty BNC (Bayonet Neill–Concelman) Connectors and low-loss shielding cable paths—undergoes strict hardware calibration routines.

Using a laboratory Spectrum Analyzer, the impedance parameters are precisely tuned to match standard 50 Ω RF network metrics. This rigorous calibration maximizes transmission power transfer efficiency, expands working frequency band parameters, and reduces insertion losses (Attenuation) and Voltage Standing Wave Ratios (VSWR).
